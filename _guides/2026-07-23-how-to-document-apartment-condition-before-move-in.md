**How to Document Apartment Condition Before Move-In** 🚨
====================================================================

Moving into a new place is exciting, but it’s also the perfect moment to protect yourself from future deposit disputes. I’ve seen too many renters get stuck paying for scratches that were already there—so let’s make sure you walk in with a clear record (and peace of mind).  

## Quick Context  
This guide is for anyone who’s just signed a lease (or is about to) and wants to create a solid move‑in record. It builds on the security‑deposit basics from Part 2 and sets you up nicely for handling maintenance requests in Part 4. Even if you’re reading this solo, the steps work whether you’re a first‑time renter or a seasoned pro.

## Gather Your Tools  
Before you even step inside, think like a detective with a checklist.  

- **Camera or smartphone** – make sure the battery’s charged and you have enough storage.  
- **Notebook or notes app** – for quick written observations.  
- **Measuring tape** – handy for noting any odd gaps or damages.  
- **Flashlight** – reveals hidden spots in closets or under cabinets.  
- **A friend (optional but helpful)** – a second pair of eyes catches things you might miss.  

> **💡 Pro Tip:** Do the walk‑through during daylight if you can; natural light shows true colors and makes shadows less deceiving.

## Do the Walk‑Through (Room by Room)  
Take your time—there’s no prize for rushing.  

1. **Start at the front door.** Note the condition of the door, frame, lock, and any paint chips.  
2. **Move clockwise** through each room (living area, kitchen, bedrooms, bathrooms).  
3. **Check walls, ceilings, and floors** for cracks, stains, water damage, or missing tiles.  
4. **Test fixtures:** flip light switches, run faucets, flush toilets, open/close windows and doors.  
5. **Look inside cabinets and closets**—shelves, rods, and doors often hide wear.  
6. **Note any appliances** (stove, fridge, dishwasher) for dents, rust, or missing parts.  

> **⚠️ Watch Out:** If something looks off, don’t assume it’s “normal wear and tear.” Document it now; you can always ask the landlord later if it’s pre‑existing.

## Capture Evidence (Photos & Notes)  
A picture really is worth a thousand words—especially when it’s timestamped.  

- **Take wide‑angle shots** of each room to show overall condition.  
- **Follow up with close‑ups** of any damage, wear, or quirks (e.g., a chipped tile, a stain on the carpet).  
- **Include a reference object** (like a coin or your hand) in close‑ups to give scale.  
- **Write a brief note** beside each photo: “Living room wall, north side – hairline crack ~2 inches, appears old.”  
- **Record a short video walk‑through** (optional) while narrating what you see; this can be super handy if a dispute arises later.  

> **🎯 Key Insight:** Timestamp your photos automatically (most phones do this) or manually write the date on a piece of paper in the shot. This removes any doubt about when the evidence was taken.

## Store and Share Your Docs  
Now that you’ve got the proof, keep it safe and accessible.  

- **Create a dedicated folder** (cloud storage like Google Drive, Dropbox, or iCloud) labeled “Move‑In Docs – [Address] – [Date].”  
- **Upload photos, videos, and your notes** there right away.  
- **Send a copy to your landlord or property manager** (email works fine) with a friendly note: “Hi [Name], please find attached our move‑in condition documentation for your records. Let me know if you’d like to discuss anything!”  
- **Keep a backup** on your phone or an external drive—just in case.  

> **💡 Pro Tip:** Ask for a read‑receipt or a quick reply confirming they got the files. That creates a paper trail showing you were proactive.

## Review with Landlord (Optional but Smart)  
If your landlord is available, do a quick joint walk‑through.  

- Point out anything you’ve noted and see if they agree it’s pre‑existing.  
- If they disagree, stay calm and refer to your timestamped evidence.  
- If you reach a verbal agreement, follow up with a short email summarizing what you both acknowledged.  

This step isn’t required everywhere, but it can prevent headaches later—and it shows you’re a responsible tenant.

## Positive Signs and Negative Signs  
Knowing what to feel good about (and what to raise an eyebrow at) helps you judge the place’s overall care.

### Positive Signs  
- Fresh paint or clean walls with only minor, expected scuffs.  
- Floors that are clean, with no major stains, warping, or loose tiles.  
- Fixtures (lights, faucets, handles) that operate smoothly and look well‑maintained.  
- No lingering odors (mold, smoke, pets) that could indicate hidden problems.  

### Negative Signs  
- Large cracks in walls or ceilings, especially if they appear recent or are widening.  
- Water stains, bubbling paint, or soft spots—possible leaks.  
- Appliances that are rusted, make strange noises, or don’t turn on.  
- Doors or windows that stick, won’t latch, or have noticeable gaps.  
- Evidence of pest droppings, nests, or chew marks.  

> **⚠️ Watch Out:** If you spot multiple negative signs in one area, consider asking for repairs before moving in—or at least get them documented in writing.

## What to Check Before You Decide  
Even after you’ve documented the condition, a few final checks can save you regret.

- **Verify that any promised repairs** (from the viewing or lease negotiation) are actually completed.  
- **Confirm utilities** are set up to start on your move‑in date (electric, gas, water, internet).  
- **Double-check lease terms** about alterations, pet policies, and subletting—so you know what you can and can’t change later.  
- **Test smoke and carbon‑monoxide detectors**—press the test button to ensure they work.  
- **Look at the building’s exterior** for obvious maintenance issues (broken railings, trash accumulation, poor lighting) that could affect your safety or comfort.  

## Key Takeaways  
- **Document early, thoroughly, and with timestamps**—photos, notes, and optionally video.  
- **Use a systematic room‑by‑room approach** so you don’t miss anything.  
- **Store your records securely and share them with the landlord** to create a mutual record.  
- **Spot the difference between normal wear and genuine red flags** before you sign anything.  
- **A quick joint walk‑through (if possible) builds trust and reduces future disputes.**
